Machine Learning Fundamentals

The Intuitive Way to Learn:
Gradient Descent

By AHMAD HASAN SAKIB • March 15, 2026

In the vast landscape of modern artificial intelligence, nearly every breakthrough—from large language models to medical diagnostic tools—relies on a singular elegant mathematical foundation: optimization. Specifically, the quest to understand how a machine can "find" the best possible answer by walking downhill.

1. The Context: Linear Regression

In machine learning, one of the simplest algorithms is Linear Regression. It is a supervised learning algorithm, meaning it learns from labeled data.

In a dataset, we usually have features (x) as our inputs and a target (y) as our output. Imagine trying to predict exam scores based on hours studied:

Hours Studied (x) Exam Score (y)

2. The Hypothesis

We assume the relationship is linear and make a guess called the hypothesis:

$$h(x) = \theta_0 + \theta_1 x$$

Here, \(\theta_0\) is the intercept and \(\theta_1\) is the weight. These parameters determine how much the feature influences the prediction. Our job is to find the best values for these weights.

3. Measuring Error: The Cost Function

To know if our model is good, we use a Cost Function. It measures how "wrong" our predictions are. For linear regression, we use Mean Squared Error (MSE):

$$J(\theta) = \frac{1}{2m}\sum_{i=1}^{m} (h_\theta(x^{(i)}) - y^{(i)})^2$$

"The goal of any learning algorithm is simple: Find the parameters that minimize this cost."

4. Enter Gradient Descent

Gradient Descent is the optimization algorithm that moves us towards that minimum. It uses an update rule:

$$\theta_{j} := \theta_{j} - \alpha \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta_{j}} J(\theta)$$
  • \(\theta_j\): The parameter we are updating.
  • \(\alpha\) (Alpha): The Learning Rate (how big our step is).
  • \(\frac{\partial J}{\partial \theta_j}\): The slope (gradient).

5. Walking Down the Hill

Imagine a hill-shaped road (a parabola). You are at the top, and you want to reach the bottom valley.

Suppose you start at x = 6. The slope is steep. You check the direction and take a step downhill. Now you are at x = 3. You are closer to the bottom, so the slope is less steep. Naturally, your next step will be smaller.

You repeat this until the slope is zero. You have reached the minimum.

6. Final Intuition

Gradient Descent is simply a smart way of saying: "Start somewhere, check which way is downhill, and keep taking steps until you reach the bottom."

The step size is governed by the steepness (gradient) and your pace (learning rate). This simple idea powers the most complex neural networks in the world today.
